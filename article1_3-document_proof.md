# The Smoking Gun Nobody Was Looking For
## Three Documents, Twelve Years, and the One-of-a-Kind Logo That Proves Lucy McKenzie Is Banksy

The proof does not begin with a stencil or a wall. It begins with a fake ad in a teenage girl's zine.

In 1995, Lucy McKenzie was a high school senior in Scotland publishing a rough photocopied magazine called Violet. Issue four contained a four-page punk-rock collage titled "Prostitute Cards" — an exposé of the escort advertisements she had collected from London phone boxes over her high school years, the best of which she had repurposed as bedroom wall decoration to her mother's notable and understandable dismay. The introduction describes them as "beautiful squares of tack." The article presents eleven of them.

It also presents a twelfth.

Tucked among the genuine ads is one McKenzie made herself. Its title is "Spanking by Angry Miss." It was assembled on a photocopier from two distinct pieces — the background shading visibly darker on one side than the other — and by virtue of that haphazard assembly, the right hand of the logo's spanked figure has been cropped off precisely at the wrist.

That excision is a one-of-a-kind mark. It cannot be attributed to stock imagery, shared sources, or coincidence. Without it, the logo is generic. With it, the logo is singular — a fingerprint pressed into the work at the moment of its making.

McKenzie's introduction openly wonders why no "artwanker" had yet repurposed escort ads into a late-night TV comedy segment. She was, at the time she wrote this, exactly the sort of artwanker made for the job — as the fake ad she had just slipped into her own article demonstrated. She simply wasn't ready to say so yet.

---

Nine years later, the logo reappears.

In late 2003 or early 2004, a single sheet of paper circulated during a working meeting between Banksy and Steve Lazarides, the Bristol photographer who had recently formalized his role as Banksy's commercial partner. The document later surfaced in Lazarides' personal archive and was sold at auction — mislabeled by the auction house as "Original Erotic Art Concept Sketches" — as Lot 51.

One side of the sheet contains meeting notes: logo and website design ideas for Pictures on Walls, Banksy's art and exhibition company, alongside a grid of line-drawn sex-work panels illustrating a colorway strategy for printed editions. The other side contains a work-in-progress revision of McKenzie's 1995 fake ad. Same title. Same logo. And the figure's hand, cropped at the wrist, at exactly the same anatomical point as in the original.

The layout has been reworked: the logo enlarged and centered, the typography more deliberate, the composition more balanced. One line of text completes the revision: "I love my job." It is the comedic caption the original ad lacked — the punchline that transforms infiltration into parody, precisely the move McKenzie's 1995 introduction had proposed. Lazarides himself, in the documentary made about the auction twenty years later, confirms on camera that Banksy was working to improve the ad during the meeting. He appears, two decades on, still visibly discomfited by the memory.

The revision is not a quotation or an homage. It is a continuation — an artist returning to an unfinished idea and completing it to specifications she had set for herself nine years earlier. The Violet zines were so obscure that I could locate only two of the five issues for purchase and obtained the remaining three by trading a limited-edition Richard Kern print to a riot grrrl scholar who had known McKenzie in those years. There is no version of events in which Banksy independently discovered a fake ad in a teenage girl's private zine, recognized its specific one-of-a-kind flaw, and then completed it to the exact conceptual specifications the original author had described in her introduction. That chain requires a single author working across time.

The cropped wrist is the fingerprint. Lot 51 is where it reappears.

---

The third document is public, polished, and pointed.

In October 2007, an auction billed as a Lazarides venture took place at the Shadow Lounge in London — a drag club. The catalogue cover is a full-bleed photographic tableau: a riot of escort advertisements, professionally lit and composed to commercial-photography standard, with trans sex-worker ads prominent throughout. It is McKenzie's 1995 "Prostitute Cards" article redone at full professional scale — the same structure, the same genre, the same fixation, now realized as a sculptural tableau photographed for reproduction.

The corporate record clarifies what the billing obscured. The auction was a Lazarides auction in name only. At the time, LazInc — Lazarides' corporate loan-out company — was being managed by Simon Durban, POW's accountant and operational director. The auction generated approximately £3 million gross for LazInc and was followed within months by a direct payment of £112,000 to Lazarides for his POW shares. It was his exit package — the mechanism by which an unwanted partner was bought out and removed. The catalogue cover advertised his departure while he was still nominally its subject.

Embedded in the cover, in yellow typeface against an orange background that renders it nearly invisible unless you are looking for it, is the line "I love my job" — placed in the body of one of the advertisements, immediately below the post-it note announcing the Lazarides auction. It is the punchline from the 2004 revision, transferred to the 2007 sequel. Only one person in the world had reason to place it there: the person who had written it on the back of a sheet of POW meeting notes three years earlier, in the presence of the man whose exit this catalogue was funding.

The 1995 article had eleven real ads and one fake. The 2007 catalogue cover holds the same ratio — eleven ad positions and one occupied by the Lazarides post-it, which takes the twelfth slot. The symmetry is exact. The only person who would know to maintain it is the person who established it thirty years ago.

---

What artists do not do is spend a decade refining another artist's unfinished adolescent idea, completing it to that artist's stated specifications, then building a twelve-year sequel to it at professional scale. That is not how influence works. That is how an artist works on their own material.

The Violet zines are too obscure to be anyone's source material but the original author's. The one-of-a-kind logo connects 1995 to 2004 with a specificity that coincidence cannot survive. The 2007 sequel is too structurally precise — the ratio, the hidden caption, the exit timing — to be the work of anyone other than the person who drew the original.

Three documents. Twelve years. One fingerprint. The case that follows runs to hundreds of pages. It is there for those who need it. This is there for those who don't.
