The Schism That Never Made the Press: How the Banksy Partnership Fractured and What the Corporate Record Says About It
There is no press release announcing the end of the founding Banksy partnership. There is no interview in which anyone describes what happened between 2006 and 2009. The myth of the lone anonymous artist does not accommodate the language of partnership dissolution, equity disputes, and management separations — so that language never appeared in the public record.
It appeared in Companies House instead.
The corporate record of the Banksy enterprise across the period 2006–2009 is a detailed account of a fractured partnership told entirely in filings, appointment dates, resignation dates, and renamed entities. Read chronologically, it describes a sequence of events that the art press never reported because none of the principals could afford to acknowledge it.

The first signal is a firing.
Steve Warren managed the Pictures on Walls retail operation and the Santa's Ghetto shows. His departure from the enterprise followed the 2006 Santa's Ghetto event in Bethlehem, a show that drew significant attention but whose logistics were widely described by participants as chaotic and whose commercial returns did not match its cultural profile. Warren was not publicly fired. He was not publicly anything. He ceased to be part of the operation and the operation continued without explanation.
The second signal is a second firing.
Ben Eine was a core member of the street art crew that provided the enterprise with its scene credibility and its workforce. His exit from the inner circle followed what has been described as an eBay backdoor print scandal — prints moving through secondary channels without POW's knowledge or authorisation, the commercial integrity of the print operation compromised by someone inside the network who understood its value and acted on that understanding privately. Eine was not publicly removed. The enterprise simply reorganised around his absence.
Two exits in the same period, neither explained, both involving the commercial apparatus of the print operation. The pattern points to a moment at which the founding partnership decided it had grown large enough to survive the removal of its working parts, and proceeded to remove the ones that had become liabilities.

The Lazarides separation is the central event, and it happened in slow motion.
The corporate record shows Simon Durban — the operational backbone of the enterprise, simultaneously a director of POW, PCO, Paranoid Pictures, GDP, and Dismaland — resigning from LazInc in March 2009. Lazarides is listed as a director of LazInc from 2004 to 2020, but Durban's departure from that entity in March 2009 is the clean separation date: the moment the Durban operational infrastructure — which was effectively the connective tissue of the whole enterprise — withdrew from the Laz side of the structure and consolidated on the POW/PCO side.
Lazarides publicly describes his departure from the Banksy operation as a mutual decision, a natural evolution, a parting of ways between professionals who had grown in different directions. The corporate record describes something more specific. Durban's five simultaneous resignations from POW, PCO, Paranoid Pictures, GDP, and Dismaland on 7 November 2019 mark the real operational wind-down date — the moment the superstructure formally ceased to be a going concern. The March 2009 Lazarides separation predates that wind-down by a decade. It is the schism date, not the dissolution date.
What happened between those two dates is the story of a partnership running on contested terms: the commercial apparatus continuing to produce, sell, and authenticate work while the underlying agreement about who was entitled to what had not been resolved. The print operation did not stop. The shows continued. The authentication certificates kept being issued. But the entity issuing them — the Pest Control Office — and the entity producing the commercial output — Pictures on Walls — were operating with a fracture in their founding relationship that neither could publicly acknowledge without destroying the brand that made both of them valuable.

The post-schism signed editions carry the schism in their signatures.
After the Lazarides separation, the signed editions that continued to enter the market were signed by the Artist of Record — Damien Hirst — rather than by the artist. This is the logical consequence of the separation. The artist's deal with the enterprise had been built around a specific founding structure. When that structure was contested, her willingness to certify the commercial output of the contested operation became conditional.
The prints she signs are the prints she claims. The prints signed by the AoR after 2009 are the commercial inventory of an operation that continued to use her brand without her unconditional imprimatur. Both sets of prints exist in the market. Both carry authentication certificates from the Pest Control Office. The certificates do not distinguish between them. The signatures do.
The Banksy Explained paper editions chart does not annotate which prints carry which signatures. It records edition sizes and AP counts. But the edition structure of the post-schism prints — the ones that continued to appear after 2009 with round-number editions and no unusual AP arithmetic — is visually consistent with commercial production under AoR authority rather than artist certification. The pointed totals, the irregular AP counts that encode distribution structures, the 666 of Choose Your Weapon: these belong to the pre-schism record. The post-schism commercial output carries cleaner numbers because the artist who made the numbers irregular was no longer in a position to do so.

The LA print run is where the schism's proximate cause is most visible.
Four of the six Barely Legal prints — Grannies, Applause, Morons, and Trolleys — had their remaining unsigned editions completed by Pictures on Walls in 2007. Festival never received a POW run. Sale Ends was deferred eleven years to a 2017 Version 2. Two prints from the same show, presented at the same event, treated differently by the commercial apparatus.
The reason is not aesthetic. The reason is that POW jumped ahead of the artist's final approval on print quality and production terms for the LA run, completing editions before the artist had certified the output. The artist's response was to withhold certification from two of the six prints: leaving Festival permanently incomplete and deferring Sale Ends until the terms of completion could be renegotiated more than a decade later. The completed four were allowed through. The contested two were not.
The LA print run is where the schism became visible in the commercial record, three years before the corporate separations made it visible in Companies House. The founding deal gave the artist final say on print quality. POW violated that clause. The artist's response was encoded in edition completions and incompletions that the Banksy Explained site dutifully records, twenty-one years later, in a table that most readers browse without counting.
